\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%<TODO:add a sentence that we are exploring why certain cell types are not predictable>
We demonstrate that jointly modeling histopathology and spatial transcriptomics through multimodal transformers yields more effective morpho-molecular representations than existing contrastive approaches. By fusing subcellular Xenium transcript readouts directly into the ViT token stream, our model captures fine-grained spatial dependencies between tissue morphology and gene expression without requiring cell segmentation. Across a comprehensive benchmark spanning gene expression and cell type composition prediction, multimodal fusion consistently outperforms unimodal baselines, with particularly strong gains on the more challenging task of predicting cellular heterogeneity.

Recent large-scale efforts have established cross-modal representation learning as a robust approach for linking histology and transcriptomics, largely through late fusion and contrastive pretraining~\cite{gindra_largescalebenchmarkcrossmodal_2025,chen_visualomicsfoundation_2025}. Our results complement these findings by showing that the granularity of transcript representations (token-level versus tile-level) can qualitatively change conclusions about fusion strategies. When targets require capturing fine-grained heterogeneity, as in cell type composition prediction, jointly processing morphology and transcript tokens throughout the transformer yields substantial improvements. This may help interpret the heterogeneous results reported across datasets and tasks in prior work~\cite{gindra_largescalebenchmarkcrossmodal_2025}.

By framing gene and cell type prediction as supervised tasks on targeted spatial transcriptomics panels, our approach provides a biologically grounded path toward multimodal foundation models. Unlike contrastive objectives that maximize global image--transcript agreement, our supervised formulation explicitly tests whether models can leverage morphology to predict fine-grained molecular phenotypes, a capability directly relevant to biomarker discovery and treatment response prediction. Critically, our model is truly multimodal at inference time, jointly processing both morphological and molecular inputs rather than relying on a single modality once deployed.

\subsection*{Limitations and Future Directions}
\label{sec:limitations-and-future-directions}

\paragraph{Dataset and generalization.}
Our evaluation is limited to a single internal cancer dataset. Generalization across tissue types, staining protocols, and acquisition settings remains a central challenge in computational pathology~\cite{jaume_hest1kdatasetspatial_2024,chen_visualomicsfoundation_2025}, and we are actively extending our evaluation to multi-center cohorts such as HEST-1k to assess cross-site robustness.

\paragraph{Architecture and fusion mechanisms.}
We deliberately restrict ourselves to lightweight fusion mechanisms (a shallow transcript token encoder with addition- or concatenation-based fusion) to isolate the effects of fusion timing and representation granularity. More expressive alternatives, such as Flamingo-style gated cross-attention~\cite{alayrac_flamingovisuallanguage_2022}, could offer finer control over how transcriptomic information modulates visual features. Our capacity ablations suggest that larger models better exploit multimodal inputs, motivating future exploration of more expressive fusion architectures and fine-tuning of pretrained H\&E foundation models.

\paragraph{Toward cell-level and sub-tile prediction.}
Our current formulation aggregates predictions over all cells within a $512 \times 512\,\mu\text{m}$ tile, potentially underutilising the spatial resolution of imaging-based spatial transcriptomics. A natural extension is prediction at the token level, where each ViT patch token independently predicts local molecular profiles, producing spatially explicit maps of gene expression and cell type identity within a tile. The finding that token-level transcript representations already improve tile-level predictions suggests that fine-grained supervision could be particularly beneficial, and we are actively developing this direction.

\paragraph{Understanding cell-type-specific failure modes.}
Our per-cell-type analysis (Section~\ref{sec:per-cell-type}) reveals characteristic failure modes for unimodal models, most notably the collapse of expression-only models on NK cells. The biological and technical mechanisms driving these failures warrant further investigation.

\paragraph{Panel design and target selection.}
Redundancy between core and add-on panels can dominate prediction performance and obscure architectural differences, as evidenced by the competitive performance of the Min-MAE baseline. Reducing core--add-on correlation while emphasising targets that require morphological context would provide a sharper stress test for multimodal fusion strategies. Deliberate panel design may also be a practically relevant consideration when planning spatial transcriptomics experiments. We are additionally evaluating our approach on target panels from other platforms such as DeepSpot~\cite{nonchev_deepspotleveragingspatial_2025}, where early results suggest consistent performance and robustness to panel choice.

\paragraph{Transfer learning and pretraining.}
Ongoing transfer learning experiments with frozen CLIP-pretrained ViT-S backbones indicate that token-level CLIP embeddings outperform tile-level pooled embeddings for gene prediction, further supporting the importance of spatial granularity in multimodal pretraining.
