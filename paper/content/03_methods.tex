In this section, we describe our approach for predicting spatial gene expression and cell type composition from histopathology images and partial transcriptomic data. We begin by detailing our data processing pipeline, including spatial alignment of H\&E images with Xenium transcriptomics and cell phenotyping procedures. We then formalize the problem setup for both prediction tasks. Next, we present our model architecture, which integrates morphological features from H\&E-stained tissue with spatial transcriptomics data through a vision transformer-based framework. Finally, we describe the baseline methods we compare against and our training and evaluation protocols.

\paragraph{Terminology.}
Throughout this chapter we use the following terms consistently. A \emph{whole-slide image} (WSI) refers to a high-resolution digital scan of a histological tissue section, typically captured at $0.25$--$0.5\,\mu\text{m}$ per pixel. A \emph{tile} is a fixed-size, spatially localized crop of the WSI; we work with $512 \times 512$ pixel tiles at $0.5\,\mu\text{m}/\text{px}$, corresponding to a $256 \times 256\,\mu\text{m}$ tissue area. Within the ViT architecture, each tile is further divided into a regular grid of non-overlapping $16 \times 16$ 
pixel sub-regions called patches. Here we use a ViT-S/B (Small or Base variant of the Vision Transformer), 
and each patch is being mapped to a \emph{token}, a $d$-dimensional vector that is the fundamental unit of computation 
in the transformer. Transcript and morphology features are both represented as token sequences, with one token 
per ViT patch position, enabling direct spatial alignment between the two modalities.

\subsection*{Data Processing}
\label{sec:data-processing}

\subsubsection{H\&E and Xenium Spatial Alignment}
\label{sec:he-and-xenium-spatial-alignment}

We register the H\&E whole-slide image (WSI) to the Xenium coordinate system using the DAPI channel and manually selected landmark correspondences. These landmarks are used to estimate an affine transformation that maps Xenium transcript coordinates into H\&E pixel space. To account for local tissue deformations, we partition each tissue section into regions and estimate a separate affine transform per region if necessary. Finally, we crop the WSI to the transcript-containing area to restrict downstream tiling to regions with Xenium measurements.

We tile each WSI into $512\times512$ pixel tiles with a stride of 256 pixels at 
a resolution of $0.5\,\mu$m per pixel, yielding a total of 344{,}328 tiles. The 
50\% overlap ensures that tissue structures near tile boundaries are captured 
across multiple tiles, reducing information loss from cells split at edges. Each 
tile is resized to $224\times224$ pixels prior to inference, after which the 
vision transformer tokenizes it into $16\times16$ pixel patches. At the resulting 
effective resolution of approximately $1.14\,\mu$m per pixel, each patch spans 
roughly $18\,\mu$m, approximately the diameter of a human lung cell
\cite{crapo_cellnumbercell_1982}.

To ensure sufficient signal for downstream modeling, we apply task-specific quality filters. For gene expression prediction, we filter tiles to contain at least 1{,}000 transcripts to ensure sufficient transcriptomic signal for models that rely on it (\autoref{fig:p-fus:num-genes-tiles}). Similarly, for cell type count prediction, we filter tiles to contain at least 20 cells to ensure adequate cellular representation for composition analysis. Cell density per tile does not correlate with whole-slide image size (\autoref{fig:p-fus:tiles-vs-density}), supporting the validity of cross-sample comparisons. This filtering step results in our final dataset of 338{,}662 tiles for analysis for the gene prediction task.

\subsubsection{Cell Phenotyping}
\label{sec:p-fus:cell-phenotyping}

Cell typing was performed by collaborators\footnote{Cell type annotations were provided by Daria Buszta, Jonathan Bac, and Senbai Kang from the Biomedical Data Science Center at CHUV.}. Cell type annotations were assigned using a reference-based deconvolution approach that leverages matched single-nucleus RNA-seq (snRNA-seq) data to accurately identify cell types while accounting for technical differences between platforms and potential signal contamination from transcript diffusion. Matched snRNA-seq data were generated from FFPE tissue sections adjacent to those used for Xenium profiling. Single-nucleus sequencing was performed using the 10x Genomics Chromium platform following standard protocols. Raw sequencing data were processed using CellRanger, quality control filters were applied to retain high-quality nuclei: cells with at least 200 detected genes and fewer than 20\% of reads mapping to mitochondrial genes were retained. Cell type labels were transferred from the snRNA-seq reference to Xenium single-cell profiles using Robust Cell Type Decomposition (RCTD)~\cite{cable_robustdecompositioncell_2022}

\paragraph{Gene Expression Prediction.}
The Xenium spatial transcriptomics panel is divided into two complementary subsets, as provided by the Xenium 
platform and discussed in \autoref{sec:from-gene-prediction-to-phenotyping}, to simulate a realistic gene 
expression prediction scenario. The first subset, termed the \emph{core panel}, comprises $G_{\text{core}}$ genes and represents the transcriptomic features that are available as input to our models. The second subset, the \emph{add-on panel}, contains $G_{\text{add}}$ genes and serves as the prediction target. This partitioning allows us to evaluate how well models can predict unmeasured gene expression using available histological and partial transcriptomic information. For each spatial location corresponding to a tile, our primary objective is to predict the \emph{add-on} gene expression vector $\mathbf{y}\in\mathbb{R}^{G_{\text{add}}}$ given the available inputs: H\&E histology and/or transcripts from the core gene panel (see \autoref{fig:p-fus:architecture}).

To construct the gene expression targets, we aggregate all add-on panel transcripts that fall within each tile's spatial boundaries. The raw transcript counts are then transformed using a log1p transformation (i.e., $\log(1 + x)$) to obtain the target vector $\mathbf{y}$. This transformation helps to stabilize variance and normalize the distribution of gene expression values, which typically exhibit high dynamic range and right-skewed distributions.
%(Figure~\ref{fig:p-fus:num-transcripts-in-tiles}.)

\paragraph{Cell Type Count Prediction.}
For each tile, we predict a vector $\mathbf{c}\in\mathbb{R}^{39}$ representing the counts of the 39 identified cell types across the cohort. This task complements gene expression prediction by directly assessing whether models can capture cellular phenotypes and their spatial distributions from histological and transcriptomic features. Cell type targets are derived from the phenotyping pipeline described in Section~\ref{sec:p-fus:cell-phenotyping} (or the subsection above). Similar to gene expression targets, we aggregate cell counts within each tile and apply a log1p transformation to obtain the final target vector $\mathbf{c}$. Tiles with more cells tend to capture greater cell type diversity as shown in (\autoref{fig:p-fus:cell-types-count}).

\subsection*{Model Architecture}
\label{sec:model-architecture}

Our architecture integrates visual and transcriptomic modalities through a vision transformer (ViT) backbone that processes spatially aligned tokens from both data types. We describe the processing of each modality and their fusion below.

\subsubsection{Image Encoding}
\label{sec:image-encoding}

Each H\&E tile undergoes preprocessing and encoding to generate morphological features. First, tiles are resized from their original $512\times512$ pixels to $224\times224$ pixels to match the input requirements of standard vision transformer architectures. Each resized tile is then processed by the vision token encoder component of a ViT, which we refer to as the \emph{Morph Encoder} (\autoref{fig:p-fus:architecture}).

The vision token encoder partitions the input image into a grid of non-overlapping patches and tokenizes them. This process yields $N$ patch tokens, plus a classification (CLS) token that provides a global image representation. The encoder outputs a sequence of token embeddings $\mathbf{H}_{\text{img}}\in\mathbb{R}^{(N+1)\times d}$, where $d$ is the embedding dimension. Individual patch-token embeddings are denoted by $\mathbf{h}_{\text{img}}^{(i)}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ for $i\in\{1,\dots,N\}$, where each embedding captures local morphological features corresponding to a specific spatial region of the patch.

\subsubsection{Transcript Encoding}
\label{sec:transcript-encoding}

To leverage the spatial transcriptomics data from the core panel, we explore two distinct representation strategies that differ in their level of spatial granularity:

\textbf{Tile-level aggregation.} In this approach, we aggregate all core panel transcripts within a tile into a single feature vector $\mathbf{x}_{\text{core}}\in\mathbb{R}^{G_{\text{core}}}$ that summarizes the overall gene expression profile of the tile. This representation is analogous to how we define prediction targets and provides a global view of the tile's transcriptomic state. However, it discards information about within-tile spatial heterogeneity of gene expression.

\textbf{Token-level aggregation.} To preserve fine-grained spatial information, we also consider a token-level representation that aligns with the ViT's patch grid. Using the spatial coordinates of each ViT patch in the original image space, we aggregate core panel transcripts that fall within each patch region. This yields a sequence of transcript feature vectors $\mathbf{x}_{\text{core}}^{(i)}\in\mathbb{R}^{G_{\text{core}}}$ for $i\in\{1,\dots,N\}$, where each vector is spatially aligned with its corresponding image patch embedding $\mathbf{h}_{\text{img}}^{(i)}$. 

These token-level transcript features are then embedded into the same dimensional space as the image tokens through a learned projection $f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$, parameterized by a multi-layer perceptron (MLP):
%
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_{\text{core}}^{(i)} = f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\text{core}}^{(i)}\right),
\end{equation}
%
where $f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} : \mathbb{R}^{G_{\text{core}}} \to \mathbb{R}^{d}$ constitutes our \emph{Expr Encoder}. We collect all encoded transcript tokens into a sequence $\mathbf{H}_{\text{core}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times d}$. This token-level representation enables the model to learn spatial correspondences between local morphology and gene expression patterns within each tile.

\subsection*{Baseline Methods}
\label{sec:baseline-methods}

To comprehensively evaluate the contributions of different modalities and fusion strategies, we benchmark a range of unimodal and multimodal baseline approaches.

\subsubsection{Morphology-Only Models (Morph-only)}
\label{sec:morphology-only-models-morph-only}

For models that rely solely on H\&E histology, we evaluate several pretrained foundation models as frozen feature extractors. These include CONCH \cite{lu_multimodalgenerativeai_2024}, a visual-language foundation model developed using diverse sources of histopathology images, biomedical text; Phikon \cite{filiot_phikonv2largepublic_2024}, a self-supervised ViT trained on 460 million pathology tiles covering more than 30 cancer sites; and the OmiCLIP/Loki image encoder \cite{chen_visualomicsfoundation_2025}, which was pretrained on multimodal histology-transcriptomics data. 

Additionally, we evaluate a ViT-S (small) backbone from the \texttt{timm} library under two training regimes: (i) frozen feature extraction, where the pretrained weights are fixed and only the prediction head is trained, and (ii) end-to-end fine-tuning, where all model parameters are updated during training. These comparisons allow us to assess the value of different pretraining strategies and the benefit of task-specific fine-tuning.

\subsubsection{Expression-Only Models (Expr-only)}
\label{sec:expression-only-models-expr-only}

For transcript-only baselines, we train lightweight models that predict either \emph{add-on} gene expression or cell type counts from core panel measurements alone.

\paragraph{Transcript representation.}
A key design choice across all transcript-based models is how the spatial structure of the core panel measurements is represented. In the \emph{tile-level} representation, all core panel transcripts within a tile are aggregated into a single vector $\mathbf{x}_{\text{core}} \in \mathbb{R}^{G_{\text{core}}}$, which is then encoded to obtain a tile-level transcript embedding $\mathbf{z}_{\text{core}} = f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{x}_{\text{core}}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$, discarding any within-tile spatial information. In the \emph{token-level} representation, transcripts are instead aggregated at the resolution of individual ViT patch positions, yielding a spatially resolved sequence $\{\mathbf{x}^{(i)}_{\text{core}}\}_{i=1}^{N}$ as described in Section~\ref{sec:transcript-encoding}. Each patch token is encoded independently by $f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and the resulting embeddings are globally averaged,
%
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_{\text{core}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\text{core}}^{(i)}\right) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbf{h}_{\text{core}}^{(i)},
\end{equation}
%
to obtain a single tile-level transcript embedding. This distinction carries through to all downstream models: tile-level representations are used in late fusion, while token-level representations are used in both the tokenized late fusion variant and early fusion (Sections~\ref{sec:late-fusion}--\ref{sec:early-fusion}).

In experiments involving late fusion (Section~\ref{sec:late-fusion}), we additionally consider an alternative transcript encoder based on Geneformer~\cite{theodoris_transferlearningenables_2023}, a pretrained transformer model for gene expression data. We use the \texttt{gf-12L-38M-i4096} checkpoint, comprising 12 transformer layers and 38 million parameters with a context length of 4096 tokens, employed as a frozen feature extractor without task-specific fine-tuning.

\subsubsection{Minimum-MAE Baseline}
\label{sec:minimum-mae-baseline}

As a simple reference method that operates purely on transcript data, establishing what can be achieved
by univariate gene-to-gene regression alone without any spatial or morphological context, we implement a 
\textbf{Min-MAE baseline}. For each target gene $t\in G_{\text{add}}$, this baseline identifies the single core gene $s^{\ast}(t)\in G_{\text{core}}$ that best predicts the target gene on the training set, as measured by mean absolute error (MAE):
\begin{equation}
s^{\ast}(t)=\arg\min_{s\in\{1,\dots,G_{\text{core}}\}}\mathrm{MAE}\!\left(y_{\cdot,t},x_{\cdot,s}\right)
\end{equation}
The prediction for gene $t$ is then simply the expression of gene $s^{\ast}(t)$ from the core panel. This is equivalent to a constrained linear model with exactly one nonzero coefficient per target gene, fixed to the value of 1. This baseline provides insight into how much information can be extracted from simple gene-gene correlations without learning complex patterns.

\subsection*{Multimodal Fusion Strategies}
\label{sec:multimodal-fusion-strategies}

We explore two primary strategies for integrating morphological and transcriptomic information: early fusion and late fusion. These approaches differ in where and how the modalities are combined in the model architecture.

\subsubsection{Early Fusion}
\label{sec:early-fusion}

Early fusion integrates transcript information directly into the ViT's token stream before the transformer layers process the combined sequence (\autoref{fig:p-fus:architecture}). This allows the model to learn joint representations and cross-modal interactions through self-attention mechanisms.

Given the image token embeddings $\mathbf{H}_{\text{img}}\in\mathbb{R}^{(N+1)\times d}$ and token-level transcript embeddings $\mathbf{H}_{\text{core}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times d}$, we test two fusion operations:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Element-wise addition:} For each spatial location $i$, we add the corresponding image and transcript token embeddings: $\mathbf{h}_{\text{fused}}^{(i)} = \mathbf{h}_{\text{img}}^{(i)} + \mathbf{h}_{\text{core}}^{(i)}$. The CLS token remains unchanged.

\item \textbf{Concatenation:} We concatenate the image and transcript token sequences along the sequence dimension. The concatenated sequence length is $2N+1$.
\end{itemize}

The resulting fused token sequence is then passed through the remaining layers of the ViT. The transformer's self-attention mechanism processes all tokens jointly, enabling the model to learn complex interactions between morphological and transcriptomic features at different spatial locations. Finally, a linear prediction head maps the CLS token to the target predictions $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ (for gene expression) or $\hat{\mathbf{c}}$ (for cell type counts).

\paragraph{Encoder capacity.}
We consider two capacity levels that are paired consistently across all models. The \emph{low capacity} configuration combines a ViT-S backbone with a single-layer \emph{Expr Encoder} MLP of 32 hidden units. The \emph{high capacity} configuration pairs a ViT-B backbone with a two-layer \emph{Expr Encoder} MLP of 128 hidden units per layer and ReLU activations.


\subsubsection{Late Fusion}
\label{sec:late-fusion}

In \emph{vanilla} late fusion approaches, each modality is processed independently through its respective encoder, and the resulting representations are combined at the embedding level before making predictions. 

Let $\mathbf{z}_{\text{img}}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ denote the image embedding (CLS token). Similarly, let $\mathbf{z}_{\text{core}}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ represent a pooled transcript embedding that summarizes the core expression profile. We test two operations:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Concatenation:} The embeddings are concatenated along the feature dimension to form $[\mathbf{z}_{\text{img}}; \mathbf{z}_{\text{core}}]\in\mathbb{R}^{2d}$, which is then passed through the prediction head.
\item \textbf{Element-wise addition:} The embeddings are summed element-wise to produce $\mathbf{z}_{\text{img}} + \mathbf{z}_{\text{core}}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Tokenized Late Fusion.}\label{per:p-fus:tokenization}
To isolate the effect of using spatially resolved versus spatially pooled transcript features (\autoref{per:p-fus:tokenization}), we introduce a tokenized variant of late fusion, for which token-level transcript embeddings $\mathbf{h}_{\text{core}}^{(i)}$ are pooled and fused with the image embedding via concatenation or addition, as described above. Comparing this variant to \emph{vanilla} late fusion allows us to assess whether preserving within-tile spatial structure during encoding improves predictive performance, even when that spatial information is ultimately discarded before prediction.


\subsection*{Contrastive Pretraining}
\label{sec:contrastive-pretraining}

To further investigate the impact of token-level versus tile-level transcript representations, we conduct \textbf{CLIP-style contrastive pretraining} \cite{radford_learningtransferablevisual_2021} experiments on our dataset. CLIP pretraining learns aligned embeddings across modalities by maximizing agreement between paired samples while minimizing agreement with non-paired samples within each minibatch.

\paragraph{Contrastive loss.}
Given a batch of $B$ paired tiles, let $\mathbf{z}_{\text{img}}^{(i)}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ and $\mathbf{z}_{\text{core}}^{(i)}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ denote the $\ell_2$-normalized image and transcript embeddings for tile $i$, respectively. We define the pairwise cosine similarity matrix $\mathbf{S}\in\mathbb{R}^{B\times B}$ with entries
\begin{equation}
  S_{ij} = \frac{{\mathbf{z}_{\text{img}}^{(i)}}^\top \mathbf{z}_{\text{core}}^{(j)}}{\tau},
\end{equation}
where $\tau > 0$ is a learnable temperature parameter. The symmetric InfoNCE loss is then
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{CLIP}} = -\frac{1}{2B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}\left[
    \log \frac{e^{S_{ii}}}{\sum_{j=1}^{B} e^{S_{ij}}}
    +
    \log \frac{e^{S_{ii}}}{\sum_{j=1}^{B} e^{S_{ji}}}
  \right].
  \label{eq:clip-loss}
\end{equation}
This objective encourages the embeddings of matched image--transcript pairs ($i=j$) to be more similar than embeddings of any non-matched pair in the batch, simultaneously from the image-to-transcript and transcript-to-image directions.

\paragraph{CLIP-tile pretraining.}
In this variant, we contrast the global image embedding $\mathbf{z}_{\text{img}}$ against the tile-aggregated core transcript embedding $\mathbf{z}_{\text{core}}$. This approach treats each tile as having a single, unified transcriptomic signature.

\paragraph{CLIP-token pretraining.}
Here, we contrast the global image embedding $\mathbf{z}_{\text{img}}$ against the mean-pooled token-level embeddings $\mathbf{h}_{\text{core}}^{(i)}$.

By comparing these two pretraining strategies, we can assess whether CLIP-style contrastive training, the dominant paradigm for multimodal pretraining~\cite{radford_learningtransferablevisual_2021}, benefits from spatially granular transcript representations.

\subsection*{Training and Evaluation}
\label{sec:training-and-evaluation}

\subsubsection{Loss Function}
\label{sec:loss-function}

All models are trained using mean squared error (MSE) as the training objective. For gene expression prediction, the loss is computed over all add-on genes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{expr}} = \frac{1}{G_{\text{add}}}\sum_{g=1}^{G_{\text{add}}} (\hat{y}_g - y_g)^2
\end{equation}
where $\hat{y}_g$ and $y_g$ are the predicted and true log1p-transformed expression values for gene $g$, respectively. For cell type count prediction, the loss is analogously computed over all cell types:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{type}} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} (\hat{c}_k - c_k)^2
\end{equation}
where $\hat{c}_k$ and $c_k$ are the predicted and true log1p-transformed counts for cell type $k$, respectively. The MSE loss is well-suited for these regression tasks and provides stable gradients during training.

\subsubsection{Training Protocol}
\label{sec:training-protocol}

All models were implemented in PyTorch and PyTorch Lightning to ensure reproducibility and consistency across experiments. Training was performed on NVIDIA A100 GPUs (40\,GB memory) using mixed-precision arithmetic (FP16) to accelerate computation and reduce memory consumption. Each model configuration was trained for up to 35 epochs, corresponding to approximately 2--7 hours of wall-clock time depending on model size and complexity. 
%(Table~\ref{tab:p-fus:model-complexity}). 
We employed early stopping based on validation performance to prevent overfitting and reduce unnecessary computation.

\paragraph{Optimization and hyperparameters.}
We used the AdamW optimizer for all experiments. Unless stated otherwise, we applied a differential learning rate strategy: $10^{-4}$ for the prediction head parameters and $10^{-5}$ for the backbone parameters. This two-tier approach allows faster adaptation of the task-specific prediction head while making more conservative updates to pretrained backbone features. Weight decay was set to $10^{-3}$ to provide moderate L2 regularization. The global batch size ranged between 256 and 512 depending on memory requirements of specific model architectures. To stabilize training, gradients were clipped to a maximum norm of 1.0.

We employed a cosine learning rate schedule with a linear warmup phase. The learning rate increased linearly from 0 to the target rate over the first 5 epochs (warmup phase), followed by cosine annealing that gradually reduced the learning rate to a minimum of $10^{-6}$ by the end of training. This schedule promotes stable optimization in early training while enabling fine-grained convergence in later epochs.

\paragraph{Data splitting and model selection.}
Data were split into fixed training, validation, and test partitions at the patient level, ensuring that all tiles from a given patient are assigned exclusively to one partition. The specific split configuration and patient-level assignments are detailed in \autoref{tab:p-fus:cohort}.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:evaluation-metrics}

Following prior work in spatial transcriptomics prediction~\cite{chen_visualomicsfoundation_2025,gindra_largescalebenchmarkcrossmodal_2025}, we compute metrics on a per-gene basis and then average across all genes in the \emph{add-on} panel. For cell type count prediction, we compute the same metrics on a per-cell-type basis and average across all cell types. Specifically, we report:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Pearson correlation coefficient:} Measures the linear relationship between predicted and true values, capturing how well the model preserves relative patterns across spatial locations.

\item \textbf{Spearman correlation coefficient:} Assesses monotonic relationships using rank-based correlation, making it robust to outliers and non-linear transformations.

\item \textbf{Mean squared error (MSE):} Quantifies the average squared deviation between predictions and ground truth, penalizing larger errors more heavily.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Dataset and Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:dataset-and-experimental-setup}

All experiments were conducted on an internal cancer dataset comprising 159 patients (153 with cell type annotations derived as described in \autoref{sec:p-fus:app-cell-phenotyping}) with paired H\&E histology and Xenium spatial transcriptomics measurements. The Xenium panel includes both the core Human Immuno-Oncology Panel and a custom add-on panel. The core panel consists of $G_{\text{core}} = 380$ genes that cover key immune and cancer-related pathways, while the add-on panel contains $G_{\text{add}} = 100$ custom genes selected for their relevance to cancer biology and treatment response of the cohort.

This dataset provides a challenging and realistic testbed for evaluating multimodal integration approaches, as it reflects the complexity and heterogeneity of tumor microenvironments.

In addition to this internal cohort, we evaluate organ-specific external cohorts derived from HEST-1k~\cite{jaume_hest1kdatasetspatial_2024}. These experiments are constructed separately for each organ-level subset, with tiles assigned to training, validation, and test partitions at the sample level to prevent leakage across splits. This design enables matched evaluation across distinct tissue contexts while preserving a consistent tile-level prediction setup.

We further evaluate on HESCAPE-style benchmark subsets derived from HEST-1k~\cite{gindra_largescalebenchmarkcrossmodal_2025}. In these experiments, cohort membership is fixed a priori and evaluation is performed over predefined outer folds rather than ad hoc random resampling. The configured cohorts include breast, bowel, lung-healthy, human-immuno-oncology, and human-multi-tissue, allowing us to assess performance across benchmark-aligned tissue collections with distinct sample composition and panel constraints.

For BEAT expression prediction, we consider both default-panel and highly variable gene (HVG) settings. In the HVG setting, the target panel is defined by the top 50 or top 100 highly variable genes selected from training data only using the Scanpy highly variable gene procedure with the \texttt{seurat\_v3} flavor and \texttt{sample\_id} as batch key~\cite{wolf_scanpylargescalesinglecell_2018}. The remaining genes shared across the training samples are used as the source panel, yielding split-specific \texttt{hvg-50} and \texttt{hvg-100} prediction tasks.
