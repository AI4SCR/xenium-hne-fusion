\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We present a comprehensive evaluation of our multimodal framework across two prediction tasks (gene expression and cell type composition prediction), three datasets (BEAT, HEST1k, HESCAPE), two gene panels (HVG-50, HESCAPE panel), and two model complexities (low, high). We additionally evaluate clinical downstream performance via MIL. Throughout, we use the model names defined in Section~\ref{sec:methods}: \textit{vision}, \textit{CONCH}, \textit{Geneformer}, \textit{expr-tile}, \textit{expr-token}, \textit{early-fusion-add}, \textit{early-fusion-concat}, \textit{late-fusion-tile-add/concat}, \textit{late-fusion-token-add/concat}.

\subsection*{Gene Expression Prediction}
\label{sec:results-gene}

\paragraph{Multimodal fusion outperforms unimodal baselines.}
\label{par:gene-fusion-outperforms}

On BEAT, multimodal fusion models outperform all unimodal baselines on gene expression prediction (\autoref{fig:p-fus:gene-lvl_2}, \autoref{tab:p-fus:gene-table}). Our best multimodal models achieve a $26.5\%$ improvement in Spearman correlation over the best vision-only baseline and a $13\%$ improvement over the best expression-only baseline, demonstrating the complementary nature of the two modalities.

\paragraph{Token-level representations drive performance gains.}
\label{par:gene-token-level}

Token-level transcript representations are a key driver of performance. \textit{late-fusion-token} variants substantially outperform their \textit{late-fusion-tile} counterparts, demonstrating that preserving within-tile spatial transcript structure during encoding is beneficial even when that spatial information is ultimately discarded before the prediction head. \textit{early-fusion} variants further benefit from joint processing of spatially aligned tokens throughout the transformer. CLIP-style contrastive pretraining experiments confirm this: CLIP-token achieves recall@1 of $0.062 \pm 0.004$ versus $0.041 \pm 0.003$ for CLIP-tile (random baseline: $0.004$), indicating that spatially resolved transcript features yield better-aligned multimodal embeddings (\autoref{tab:p-fus:clip-results}).

\paragraph{Task-specific training outperforms frozen foundation models.}
\label{par:gene-task-specific}

Lightweight, task-specific models consistently outperform frozen foundation model embeddings. A low-capacity \textit{vision} model trained end-to-end achieves higher correlations than linear probes on frozen \textit{CONCH} or \textit{Geneformer} features, suggesting that task-specific adaptation to morpho-molecular relationships in spatial transcriptomics outweighs large-scale pretraining benefits.

\paragraph{Comparison to Min-MAE baseline.}
\label{par:gene-minmae}

Both early and late fusion models clearly outperform the Min-MAE baseline across all genes and functional modules (\autoref{fig:p-fus:main-results-minmae}). The \textit{expr-tile} model performs comparably to Min-MAE, indicating that its predictions are largely driven by simple cross-panel gene--gene correlations. The \textit{vision} model is outperformed by Min-MAE across almost all genes, confirming that morphology alone provides limited information for predicting specific gene expression without transcriptomic context.

\subsection*{Cell Type Composition Prediction}
\label{sec:results-cell}

\paragraph{Early fusion achieves the strongest gains.}
\label{par:cell-early-fusion}

Multimodal fusion achieves even stronger gains on cell type composition prediction than on gene expression prediction (\autoref{tab:p-fus:cell-table}, \autoref{fig:p-fus:cell-lvl_2}). \textit{early-fusion-add} consistently outperforms all other model variants, substantially outperforming late fusion approaches. We attribute this to the inherent difficulty of the task: accurately reproducing cellular heterogeneity requires disentangling distinct phenotypes and their relative abundances, a task that benefits from fine-grained integration of morphological and molecular features at the token level.

\paragraph{Token-level representations are critical.}
\label{par:cell-token-level}

\textit{late-fusion-token} variants outperform \textit{late-fusion-tile} counterparts for cell type prediction, consistent with the gene expression task. Token-level spatial structure captures the within-tile co-expression patterns that define cell identity, which are averaged out in tile-level aggregation.

\paragraph{Model capacity selectively benefits multimodal models.}
\label{par:cell-capacity}

Increasing model capacity (from low to high) consistently improves multimodal and expression-only models but yields no gains for vision-only baselines (\autoref{fig:p-fus:high-low-capacity}). For \textit{early-fusion-add}, the high-capacity configuration (ViT-B) achieves mean Pearson $0.761$ versus $0.688$ for the low-capacity configuration (ViT-S), while \textit{vision} at both capacity levels performs comparably ($0.554$ vs.\ $0.541$--$0.543$). For expression-only models, increasing depth improves performance from $0.576$ to $0.655$. These results implicate cross-modal interaction rather than model size as the driver of performance.

\paragraph{Core panel input is sufficient for cell type prediction.}
\label{par:cell-panel}

Models trained on the full or core panel substantially outperform those using only the add-on panel, while the gap between full- and core-panel models is modest (\autoref{fig:p-fus:cell-panel}). This is consistent with the biological design of the panels: the core Human Immuno-Oncology Panel covers established immune and cell-type-specific markers directly informative for cell identity. Multimodal models consistently outperform expression-only counterparts regardless of panel, and the morphological advantage is largest when only the add-on panel is available.

\paragraph{Per-cell-type analysis reveals characteristic unimodal failure modes.}
\label{sec:per-cell-type}

Aggregate correlation statistics can mask important per-cell-type variation. \autoref{fig:p-fus:sample-level-pearson} shows per-cell-type Pearson correlations for \textit{early-fusion-add} across all 39 cell types. Performance is high and consistent for abundant cell types, with 14 of the top 15 most abundant populations exceeding $0.8$, and declines for rarer populations.

\textit{early-fusion-add} achieves high and consistent correlations across the majority of cell types (\autoref{fig:p-fus:cell-type-level}). Unimodal models exhibit two characteristic failure patterns:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Both unimodal models fail, but their combination substantially outperforms either alone: regulatory T cells, ciliated epithelial, club, and mesothelial cells.
  \item Expression-only specifically fails while vision retains reasonable signal: NK cells, type 1 interferon response T cells. Most strikingly, plasmacytoid dendritic cells show near-perfect correlations for both \textit{early-fusion-add} and \textit{vision} (${\sim}0.95$) while \textit{expr-tile} collapses to ${\sim}0.60$, indicating that their identity is strongly encoded in morphology but poorly represented in the core transcriptomic panel.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Spatial Predictions}
\label{sec:results-spatial}

Spatially, our predictions closely mirror the true measurements (\autoref{fig:p-fus:true-vs-predicted}, \autoref{fig:p-fus:cell-type-spatial-pred}), capturing rich morpho-molecular structures of the tumor microenvironment. UMAP visualizations of the learned embeddings (\autoref{fig:p-fus:umaps}) show that \textit{early-fusion-add} captures transcript-driven heterogeneity while remaining spatially coherent, whereas \textit{late-fusion} closely mirrors \textit{vision} patterns.
